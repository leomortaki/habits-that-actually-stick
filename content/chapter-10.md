# Chapter 10: When to Quit a Habit

This might be the most unpopular thing I'll say: **not all habits are worth keeping.**

We've spent this whole book talking about how to build habits that stick. But there's another skill that's just as important: knowing when to let go.

I spent three years trying to build a morning journaling habit. Three years. Every morning I'd tell myself "today I'll journal." Every morning I'd fail. I'd feel guilty, try harder, fail again.

Finally, I asked myself: why am I doing this? The answer was embarrassing: because someone on the internet said journaling was the key to success.

It wasn't my goal. It wasn't serving me. I was just following someone else's blueprint.

## Not All Habits Are Worth Keeping

Ask yourself:

- Does this habit actually move me toward a goal I care about?
- Does it add or energy drain it?
- Am I doing this because I want to, or because I think I should?
- Would I miss this if it disappeared?

If you're doing something out of guilt, obligation, or because "successful people do this," that's a red flag. Not every habit that works for someone else will work for you.

## The 6-Month Evaluation

Every six months, I do a habit audit. I look at every habit I'm tracking and ask:

1. Is this still serving me?
2. Have the circumstances that led to this habit changed?
3. Is this something I do, or something I keep trying to do?

This is brutal honesty. And it's necessary.

After six months, some habits have become automatic. Some never will. Some made sense at the time but don't anymore. Some were experiments that failed.

The goal isn't to keep everything forever. The goal is to keep what's working.

## Bad Habits Disguised as Good Ones

Watch out for these:

- **Productivity over everything.** Being busy isn't the same as being effective. Sometimes the "productive" habit is just a way to avoid what actually matters.

- **Optimization as avoidance.** Spending hours reading about habits instead of actually doing them. Researching the perfect app instead of using any app. "Preparing" instead of "doing."

- **Rigid discipline at the cost of joy.** If your habits make your life miserable, something's wrong. The point is a better life, not a more optimized prison.

- **"Good" habits that hurt relationships.** Being so focused on your morning routine that you ignore your partner. Saying no to every social event because of your schedule. That's not discipline — it's isolation.

## Quitting Gracefully

When you decide to quit a habit:

1. **Don't make it a production.** You don't need to announce it. You don't need an explanation. Just stop.

2. **Don't call it failure.** This was information. You learned something. Now you're making room for something better.

3. **Replace it, don't just remove it.** If you're quitting something, have something else ready. Not another habit — just space to breathe and figure out what actually matters.

4. **Let go of the guilt.** Quitting one thing means you're saying yes to something else. That's not weakness. That's prioritization.

> **REAL TALK:** I used to think quitting was giving up. Now I see it as editing. You're cutting the scenes that don't serve the story. Your life is the movie — make it one worth watching.
