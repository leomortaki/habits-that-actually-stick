# Chapter 9: Habits for Specific Goals

By now you understand the principles. But maybe you're thinking: "That's great, but how do I actually apply this to what I want to achieve?"

Let me break it down for the most common goals.

## Fitness Habits That Stick

Here's what most people get wrong about fitness: they focus on the big goal (lose weight, build muscle, run a marathon) instead of the tiny daily behaviors that make those outcomes possible.

**Start with movement, not exercise.** Don't call it working out. Call it "moving my body" or "doing something active." This removes the pressure of "having a workout."

**The two-minute rule:** On days when you don't want to exercise, just put on your workout clothes. That's it. If you only do that, you've still won. Often, putting on the clothes leads to actually doing something.

**Stack it:** After I brush my teeth, I do 10 pushups. After I pour my morning coffee, I stretch for 2 minutes. No thinking required. The trigger does the work.

**Make it easy to start:** Lay out clothes the night before. Keep your gym bag in your car. Have a 10-minute home workout ready for days when you can't get to the gym.

## Learning/Reading Habits

**Start with page count, not time.** "Read for 30 minutes" sounds doable until you're not in the mood. "Read 10 pages" is specific and completion-focused.

**Keep the book where you wait.** In your bag, by your bed, on your phone (if that's how you read). Whenever you have 5 minutes waiting, read.

**Don't force yourself to finish books you hate.** This is controversial, but I don't finish books I don't enjoy. Life's too short. This removes the dread and makes reading feel like pleasure, not homework.

**The highlight method:** When reading non-fiction, highlight or note one idea per chapter. This gives you a "output" even if you don't discuss the book with anyone. It also helps retention.

## Money Habits

**Automate everything.** This is the single most powerful money habit. Set up automatic transfers to savings, automatic bill pay, automatic investing. Remove the decision from your hands.

**Track one number.** Not every expense, not a complex budget. Just one number. For me, it's "amount in savings." For you, it might be "net worth" or "debt paid off." One number you check weekly.

**The 24-hour rule.** For non-essential purchases over a certain amount (whatever feels significant to you), wait 24 hours. This stops impulse buying better than any willpower ever could.

**Start microscopic with saving.** Save $1 a day. Then $2. Then $5. The habit comes first; the amount grows over time.

## Relationship Habits

**The daily check-in.** One text, one message, one moment of connection per day with the important people in your life. Not a long conversation. Just "thinking of you" or "how'd it go?"

**The weekly priority.** Schedule one meaningful interaction per week. Coffee, call, walk. Put it in your calendar like any other appointment.

**The "no-phones" rule.** Designate device-free times — dinner, the first 30 minutes of getting home, Saturday morning. Presence is the habit.

**Ask better questions.** "How are you?" gets "Fine." Try "What's the best thing that happened this week?" or "What's something you're looking forward to?" This changes the quality of your relationships.

> **REAL TALK:** I used to think I needed to have my entire life optimized. Now I focus on one domain at a time. This quarter, I'm prioritizing fitness. Next quarter, maybe I'll work on money. Trying to improve everything at once is a recipe for nothing.
